Why do certain audio frequencies sound good together?

When we pluck a string, play a trumpet or strike a tune we don't just get a single frequency, otherwise it would sound like this: 

(a = {SinOsc.ar(400)}.play;)
(a.free;) // For convenience

We actually get a lot of frequencies, all integer multiples of the fundamental frequency (e.g. 400hz) which make up the harmonics of that note. If we plucked the string in the picture to the right, we would have our fundamental frequency (or 1st harmonic) at the top followed by the 2nd harmonic, 3rd harmonic and so on with the 7th harmonic at the bottom. A plucked string is vibrating at all of these frequencies simultaneously.

Notice down at the bottom of the picture the wavelength ratios 1/7, 1/6, 1/5, 1/4, 2/7, 1/3, and so on. Each of these ratios correspond with a wavelength that shares harmonics with the fundamental frequency.

For example, if our fundamental frequency is 400hz, the ratio of 2/3 of the wavelength is 3/2 of the frequency, so we get the frequency 600hz. When we consider the harmonics of each we see they overlap on every third harmonic of 400 and every second harmonic of 600.

Harmonic frequencies of 400: 400, 800, 1200, 1600, 2000, 2400, ...

Harmonic frequencies of 600: 600, 1200, 1800, 2400, 3000, ...

Let's listen to those two frequencies together: 

(
  var ratio = 3.0 / 2.0;
  a = {SinOsc.ar(400)}.play;
  b = {SinOsc.ar(400 * ratio)}.play;
)

(a.free;
 b.free;) // For convenience

If we look at a different wavelength ratio like 4/7, we get the frequency 700hz, 7/4 of the fundamental frequency of 400hz. In this case every seventh harmonic of 400 and every fourth harmonic of 700 are shared.

Harmonic frequencies of 400: 400, 800, 1200, 1600, 2000, 2400, 2800 ...

Harmonic frequencies of 700: 700, 1400, 2100, 2800, 3500, 4200, ...

Let's listen again: 

(
  var ratio = 7.0 / 4.0;
  a = {SinOsc.ar(400)}.play;
  b = {SinOsc.ar(400 * ratio)}.play;
)

(a.free;
 b.free;) // For convenience

This doesn't sound as agreeable as our 2/3 ratio. If we try many more ratios, we see that the larger the numbers in our irreducible ratio are, the more the dissonant they are. This gives us a measurement on how good frequencies sound together and we can construct a scale of notes to work with.
